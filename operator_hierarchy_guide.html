Who Goes First? Understanding Order of Operations

Why This Matters

Just like in math class with PEMDAS, Python follows a strict order of operations. When Python looks at a long line of code, it needs to know which part to calculate first. This is called operator precedence or operator hierarchy.

Example: 10 + 4 * 2 gives 18, not 28, because multiplication happens before addition!

üîç Deep Dive: Examples & Explanations

A. Arithmetic Precedence (Powers ‚Üí Multiply/Divide ‚Üí Add/Subtract)

Rule: Powers first, then multiplication/division, then addition/subtraction.

result = 10 + 4 * 2 ** 3
Step-by-Step Evaluation:
1
Exponentiation (**): 2 ** 3 becomes 8
Current expression: 10 + 4 * 8
2
Multiplication (*): 4 * 8 becomes 32
Current expression: 10 + 32
3
Addition (+): 10 + 32 becomes 42
Final Result: 42

B. Comparison & Logical Operators

Rule: Comparisons happen BEFORE logical operators. and happens before or.

score = 85
is_passing = score >= 70 and score <= 100
Step-by-Step Evaluation:
1
Comparisons: Python checks score >= 70 (becomes True) and score <= 100 (becomes True)
Current expression: True and True
2
Logical and: True and True becomes True
Final Result: True ‚Äî The score IS passing!

C. A Complex Expression (Putting It All Together)

Rule: Arithmetic ‚Üí Comparisons ‚Üí Logical operators

x = 5
y = 10
result = x + 2 * 3 > 10 and y == 10
Step-by-Step Evaluation:
1
Exponentiation & Multiplication: 2 * 3 becomes 6
Current expression: x + 6 > 10 and y == 10
2
Addition: 5 + 6 becomes 11
Current expression: 11 > 10 and 10 == 10
3
Comparisons: 11 > 10 becomes True and 10 == 10 becomes True
Current expression: True and True
4
Logical and: True and True becomes True
Final Result: True

D. Identity (is) vs. Equality (==)

== (Equality)

Checks if the values look the same.

Example:

list1 = [1, 2, 3]
list2 = [1, 2, 3]
print(list1 == list2)
# True!

Both lists contain 1, 2, 3

is (Identity)

Checks if they are the exact same object in computer memory.

Example:

list1 = [1, 2, 3]
list2 = [1, 2, 3]
print(list1 is list2)
# False!

They are different lists in different memory locations

When to Use Each:

  • == for values: "Does this list contain [1, 2, 3]?"
  • is for objects: "Is this the exact same list object?"
  • Common use: if x is None: (checking if something IS None, not just equals None)
